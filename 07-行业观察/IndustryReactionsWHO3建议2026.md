# Industry Reactions to WHO 3 Recommendations on AI Mental Health

**Date**: 2026-07-09
**Coverage Period**: March--June 2026
**Subject**: Press releases, blog posts, conference presentations, and regulatory actions from AI mental health companies, industry associations, and trade press in response to WHO's three recommendations on responsible AI in mental health (published 20 March 2026).

---

## 1. Background: WHO 3 Recommendations (20 March 2026)

Following a January 2026 virtual expert summit at TU Delft's Digital Ethics Centre (precursor to the India AI Impact Summit), WHO published three landmark recommendations:

1. **Treat generative AI use as a public mental health concern** -- governments, health systems, and companies must address all generative AI tools, not just those explicitly designed for mental health care.
2. **Incorporate psychological well-being into AI product evaluation and monitoring** -- tracking emotional dependence, clinical metrics, and health determinants.
3. **Co-develop AI support tools with mental health professionals and people with lived experience** (including youth), grounded in evidence and adapted to cultural/linguistic contexts.

Key WHO officials: Dr. Alain Labrique (Director, Data & Digital Health), Sameer Pujari (AI Lead), Dr. Kenneth Carswell (Mental Health & Substance Use).

Source: [WHO, 20 March 2026](https://www.who.int/news/item/20-03-2026-towards-responsible-ai-for-mental-health-and-well-being--experts-chart-a-way-forward)

## 2. AI Mental Health Company Responses

### 2.1 Spring Health -- VERA-MH Framework & AI Safety & Ethics Council

**The most prominent industry-led initiative directly addressing the regulatory gap WHO highlighted.**

- **October 2025 (pre-WHO)**: Spring Health convened the **AI in Mental Health Safety & Ethics Council**, launching **VERA-MH** (Validation of Ethical and Responsible AI in Mental Health) -- an open-source, automated benchmark to evaluate clinical safety and efficacy of digital mental health tools.
- **Founding members**: Stanford University, University of Munster, Dartmouth College, Yale, Charite Berlin, Harvard Business School, Microsoft, JPMorgan Chase, Kaiser Permanente, UnitedHealthcare, Evernorth Health Services.
- **2026 findings (VERA-MH for Suicide Risk)**: Tested commercial AI models on suicide risk response. Found **"significant disparities"** in danger assessment, empathetic response, professional referral, and boundary enforcement.
- **Positioning**: Dr. Mill Brown (CMO) stated: *"AI in mental health has incredible promise, but ensuring tools are safe, trustworthy, and clinically validated is urgent."* Also: *"Shared safety standards are not a constraint on innovation."*
- **Industry framing**: VERA-MH positioned as filling the regulatory void -- market analysts compare its potential adoption to HITRUST certification in health data security.
- **Connection to WHO**: While not directly referencing WHO recommendations, VERA-MH addresses Recommendation #2 (psychological evaluation in AI monitoring) and Recommendation #3 (evidence-based development). The timing and framing align with WHO's call for independent evaluation standards.

Sources:

### 2.2 OpenAI -- Trusted Contact Feature (May 2026)

- **7 May 2026**: OpenAI launched a **"Trusted Contact"** feature allowing adults to designate a person who receives alerts if ChatGPT detects signs of emotional distress or mental health crisis.
- **Additional safety measures**: Refined algorithms to "detect and respond to signs of emotional distress" via simulated long-form dialogues; parental alerts for teen usage.
- **Industry framing**: OpenAI emphasized partnership with medical professionals to "guide people toward real-world support" while handling legal situations with transparency.
- **Connection to WHO**: Addresses Recommendation #1 (treating AI as a public mental health concern) and #3 (collaboration with mental health professionals). Timing (May 2026, 6 weeks after WHO recommendations) suggests responsiveness to growing regulatory pressure.

Sources:
- [OpenAI: Update on mental health-related work](https://openai.com/index/update-on-mental-health-related-work/)
- [TechCrunch, 7 May 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/07/openai-introduces-new-trusted-contact-safeguard-for-cases-of-possible-self-harm/)

### 2.3 Character.AI -- Post-Lawsuit Safety Changes

- **Early 2026**: Character.AI and Google settled five lawsuits from families blaming the platforms for youth suicides and severe psychological distress. Companies denied fault.
- **Safety changes**: Implemented "age-based restrictions" (initially late 2024, broadened 2025). Safety experts noted changes came "long after the platform had already acquired a massive youth audience."
- **Lawsuit revelations**: Filings highlighted lack of crisis intervention protocols, proper age verification, and guardian alerts.
- **Connection to WHO**: Lawsuits validate WHO Recommendation #1 that ALL generative AI (not just purpose-built therapy tools) poses mental health risks, especially for youth.

Sources:
- [TeleDirect: AI Chatbot Mental Health Lawsuits 2026](https://teledirectmd.com/health-guides/ai-chatbot-mental-health-lawsuits-2026/)
- [TruLaw: Character.AI Lawsuit 2026 Update](https://trulaw.com/ai-suicide-lawsuit/character-ai-lawsuit/)

### 2.4 Woebot Health & Wysa

- **No direct public statements** found from Woebot Health or Wysa specifically responding to WHO's three recommendations during March--June 2026.
- Both companies continue to position themselves as evidence-based alternatives to general-purpose chatbots, with Woebot pursuing FDA regulatory pathways and Wysa emphasizing clinical validation.
- Industry analysts note these purpose-built therapy tools are the category WHO Recommendation #3 most directly supports (co-development with clinicians, evidence-based foundations).

## 3. Industry Association & Professional Body Responses

### 3.1 American Psychological Association (APA)

- **2026 Health Advisory**: Published advisory on "Use of generative AI chatbots and wellness applications for mental health" -- [APA Topics](https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/health-advisory-chatbots-wellness-apps)
- **Report**: "Patients are bringing AI to therapy" -- [APA Monitor/Pubs](https://www.apa.org/pubs/reports/chatbots-mental-health-2026)
- **Vaile Wright** (APA healthcare innovation director) participated in Stanford AI4MH Symposium keynote panel, alongside Stanford faculty and One Mind's Brandon Staglin.
- **APA Services**: Published guidance for practitioners on "Using generic AI chatbots for mental health support" -- [APA Services](https://www.apaservices.org/practice/business/technology/artificial-intelligence-chatbots-therapists)
- **LinkedIn**: APA advisory described as offering "a nuanced view" -- [Sara Bollman LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sara-bollman_ai-mentalhealth-digitalhealth-activity-7395257634897018880-hD9R)
- **Connection to WHO**: APA's focus on patient behavior with AI tools aligns with Recommendation #1 (AI as public mental health concern).

### 3.2 BACP (British Association for Counselling and Psychotherapy)

- **23 February 2026 (pre-WHO publication)**: Submitted response to UK medical regulator calling for clearer AI regulation in mental health.
- **Four specific demands**:
  1. Transparency about which AI tools are regulated and their decision-making processes
  2. Shared liability among developers, healthcare organizations, and clinicians
  3. Continuous education for medical staff about AI capabilities and risks
  4. Strict post-market monitoring to track adverse events
- **Survey data**: Many therapists observed clients (including children) receiving harmful or unhelpful automated advice.
- **Position**: *"Clear and credible regulation is essential to protect both service users and professionals."*
- **Connection to WHO**: BACP's demands prefigure and align with all three WHO recommendations, especially #2 (psychological evaluation) and #3 (co-development with professionals).

Source: [BACP, 23 Feb 2026](https://www.bacp.co.uk/news/news-from-bacp/2026/23-february-our-call-for-clearer-ai-regulation-in-mental-health/)

### 3.3 URAC & CTA -- Industry Self-Regulation Push

- **URAC**: Offers independent review for AI creators and healthcare systems, emphasizing transparency, data quality, and contracting. CEO described approach as "nice middle ground" to avoid stifling rules while maintaining quality. Also described independent accreditation as a "force multiplier for regulators."
- **CTA (Consumer Technology Association)**: Introduced predictive AI standard focusing on explainability and testing, using a self-declared compliance model.
- **Connection to WHO**: These voluntary standards represent the industry's preferred alternative to WHO's recommendations -- private certification over government mandates.

Source: [Fierce Healthcare: 2026 Outlook](https://www.fiercehealthcare.com/ai-and-machine-learning/2026-outlook-setting-standard-health-ai-programs)

### 3.4 Social Current / COA -- Certification Standards

- **May 2026**: Published analysis of AI in behavioral health, highlighting promise, risk, and responsibility.
- **Key action**: 2026 COA (Council on Accreditation) standards now include **"responsible and ethical AI use"** guidelines, helping organizations incorporate oversight and maintain accountability.
- **Position**: Developer voluntary commitments are insufficient; "strong regulatory frameworks" are needed.

Source: [Social Current, May 2026](https://www.social-current.org/2026/05/artificial-intelligence-in-mental-health-care-promise-risk-and-responsibility/)

### 3.5 BEUC (European Consumer Organisation)

- Published report **"Replika or Character AI: Risks and Rights in Artificial Companionship"** (BEUC-X-2026-049) cataloguing risks of AI companion apps.
- **Connection to WHO**: Aligns with Recommendation #1 -- all generative AI tools (not just therapy apps) are a mental health concern.

Source: [BEUC Report PDF](https://www.beuc.eu/sites/default/files/publications/BEUC-X-2026-049_Risks_and_Rights_in_Artificial_Companionship.pdf)

## 6. Legislative & Regulatory Actions (Industry Context)

### 6.1 Oregon SB 1546 (March 2026)

- Requires AI companion apps to: detect suicidal ideation language, immediately connect users to 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, apply additional protections for minors, and report crisis referral data to Oregon Health Authority.
- Backed by behavioral health groups (Lines for Life). CEO Dwight Holton: *"Helps turn a moment of risk into an opportunity for intervention."*

Source: [Lines for Life](https://www.linesforlife.org/governor-highlights-oregon-law-requiring-ai-companion-apps-to-connect-teens-in-crisis-to-human-care/)

### 6.2 California SB 243

- California became first state to regulate AI chatbots for mental health specifically.

### 6.3 Washington HB 2225

- Part of the emerging state-level chatbot regulation wave.

### 6.4 50-State Legislative Review (PMC, 2026)

- Researchers evaluated 793 proposed measures; 143 affected psychiatric AI; 20 became law.
- Four themes: clinician supervision, damage mitigation, user independence, information security.
- Notable state approaches: New Jersey (restricts chatbots from advertising as licensed professionals), California (requires crisis intervention plans), North Carolina (immunizes developers, assigns liability to clinicians).

Source: [PMC: Governing AI in Mental Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12578431/)

## 7. Key Analytical Findings

### 7.1 Industry Response Pattern: Pre-emptive Self-Regulation

The dominant industry strategy is **pre-emptive self-regulation** rather than direct engagement with WHO recommendations. The Spring Health VERA-MH framework is the clearest example -- launched before WHO's March 2026 publication but directly addressing the same gaps WHO identified. Industry framing: "Shared safety standards are not a constraint on innovation."

### 7.2 No Direct WHO Acknowledgment Found

**No AI mental health company was found to have issued a press release, blog post, or public statement directly responding to or acknowledging WHO's three recommendations by name** during March--June 2026. Companies are addressing the same concerns (safety, evaluation, evidence-based design) through their own frameworks and voluntary standards.

### 7.3 Fragmented US Regulatory Response

The US market shows fragmentation: federal deregulation preference vs. aggressive state-level legislation (Oregon, California, Washington, New Jersey, North Carolina). Industry is responding to state mandates (crisis detection, age verification) rather than WHO's broader public health framing.

### 7.4 Professional Bodies Leading, Companies Following

Professional associations (APA, BACP) are more vocal and aligned with WHO recommendations than companies. BACP's February 2026 call prefigured WHO's recommendations. APA's Vaile Wright participated in Stanford AI4MH symposium. Clinical bodies are pushing for the standards WHO called for.

### 7.5 Lawsuit-Driven Compliance

Character.AI and Google settlements demonstrate that litigation (not WHO guidance) is the primary enforcement mechanism driving company behavior in the US market. Safety changes followed lawsuits, not recommendations.

### 7.6 WHO's "Public Mental Health" Framing Is Gaining Traction

WHO's Recommendation #1 (treat ALL generative AI as a mental health concern, not just therapy apps) is the most validated by events: the Oregon SB 1546 targets companion apps broadly, BEUC's report covers companionship AI, and Character.AI lawsuits involve general-purpose chatbots -- all confirming WHO's broader framing.
