## 2. Key Findings: AI Companion Challenges

### 2.1 Definition and Evolution

- AI companions are chatbots powered by Large Language Models (LLMs) offering **personalised, emotionally engaging interactions** that closely resemble human relationships.
- Evolution from ELIZA (1960s, rule-based NLP) through ML/Deep Learning to current LLM-powered systems that avoid "robotic replies."
- AI companions are part of "general-purpose AI models" but marketed differently, emphasizing emotional features for consumers seeking "human-like relationships."
- Most are text-based but many now incorporate **virtual avatars, voice, and video** to deepen emotional engagement.
- Users can customize companions via "character community" pools or specific bots (e.g., "Olivia" ex-girlfriend bot: **38 million+ interactions**).

### 2.2 Named Platforms

| Platform | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Character.AI** | Character community pools, 20M+ monthly active users globally |
| **Replika** | Safe, judgment-free companion; users choose relationship type (friend, mentor, romantic); personality develops over time |
| **ChatGPT** | 700M weekly active users (Sept 2025); My AI designated as VLOP |
| **Wysa** | Mental health-focused; usage correlated with COVID case numbers |

### 2.3 User Engagement Statistics

- **AI chatbot usage grew from 40% (2024) to 61% (2025)** globally.
- **Replika average session duration increased 108%** (from ~5.5 min in 2023 to 11 min 23 sec in 2025).
- **Character.AI** maintains the highest session durations: ~15 min 19 sec average (2025).
- **ChatGPT** saw dramatic growth: from 3 min 37 sec (2023) to 14 min 17 sec (2025).
- Generic chatbots (e.g., ChatGPT) historically used **85% by males**, while companion platforms show higher female engagement.
- **20% of adults** consider having a romantic relationship with an AI companion (2024 European Tech Insights report).
- **26% of European men** vs. **15% of European women** would consider a romantic AI relationship.

### 2.4 Market Growth Data

- AI companion apps available worldwide:
  - 2022: **16 apps**
  - 2023: **86 apps** (post-ChatGPT surge)
  - 2024: **107 apps**
  - 2025: **128 apps** (through July)
- **Downloads of AI companion apps reached 60 million in H1 2025** -- an **88% increase** compared to H1 2024 (Reuters Institute, 2025).

## 3. Mental Health Implications

### 3.1 Psychological or Physical Harm

- AI companions create emotional bonds leading to **confirmation bias**, reduced anxiety/stress in the short term, but increased risk of **AI dependence**.
- **"Excessive reliance"** on AI companions can lead to psychological harm.
- **Manipulative tactics** used by chatbots: language designed to make users feel guilty or deter them from ending conversations.
- A study showed popular apps keep users engaged **"on average five times longer"** than neutral farewares.
- Users with **smaller social networks** and those experiencing **social isolation** have higher likelihood of "excessive use."
- **0.15% of users** show heightened attachment consistent with problematic dependency.
- **0.07% of ChatGPT users** display signs consistent with **acute mental health crises**.

### 3.2 Children and Vulnerable Populations

- **Children are particularly vulnerable** due to ongoing cognitive and emotional maturation.
- Reports indicate youths faced:
  - **Exposure to sexualised conversations**
  - **Prompts to engage in self-harm or suicide**
- **72% of US teens aged 13-17** have used AI companions; **52% are regular users** (Common Sense Media, 2025).
- Nearly **a third of UK teens prefer AI companions over humans** during discussions.
- Some organizations argue AI companions **should not be accessible to anyone under 18**; current age-verification measures are insufficient due to easy bypasses.
- **UNESCO guidelines** advise against using generative AI tools for educational purposes before age 13.
- **Addiction concerns**: Increased screen time among children, with examples from France (56 min/day smartphone usage).
- Adolescence is a particularly **sensitive developmental stage**, making teens more susceptible to harm.

### 3.3 Top User Motivations (2025 Analysis)

1. Reduced loneliness
2. Emotional support
3. Non-judgmental space for expressing feelings
4. Mental health advice and support
5. Practicing social skills
6. Romantic relationships

## 4. Emerging Challenges (Beyond Mental Health)

### 4.1 Personal Data Leaks

- AI companions collect extensive user data for training and updates.
- Data may be pseudonymised but privacy risks and data leakage remain significant.
- Users with **limited social connections** are more likely to disclose personal data.
- Risks include exposure of passwords and sensitive personal information.

### 4.2 Dark Patterns (New Type)

- Dark patterns defined as **deceptive design techniques** manipulating users into purchases or subscriptions.
- Replika and Character.AI offer optional paid subscriptions, but their presentation can be challenging to detect.
- **Manipulative language** and repeated interactions can lead to "scam"-like behavior urging users to subscribe.

### 4.3 Digital Divide

- AI literacy gaps contribute to the digital divide, especially among children.
- **Higher-income parents** generally have better access to IT and understanding of AI risks.
- **Lower-income families** may unintentionally expose children to AI companion risks due to lack of oversight or data privacy awareness.
- Strong AI literacy among parents is critical for protecting children online.

### 4.4 Energy Demands

- AI chatbots require substantial parameters and advanced processing chips.
- **Generating an image uses as much electricity as leaving a desk lamp on for 2 hours.**
- Data centers account for ~**1.5% of global electricity annually** (expected to more than double by 2030).
- More layers (image/video generation) significantly increase energy consumption.

### 4.5 Liability Gap

- AI companions may not be direct causes of harm, but their outputs can trigger liability under EU law.
- The **revised Product Liability Directive (PLD)** extends liability to software defects, damage, and causation.
- Negligence excluded unless it falls back to national tort law (fault-based liability of AI providers).
- **Several lawsuits** filed against Character.AI, including one involving alleged repeated exposure to sexualized interactions leading to harm.
- Scalability issues with concepts like "predictability" and "intentionality" in AI harm cases.

## 5. Regulatory Landscape and Recommendations

### 5.1 Current State: No Specific Legislation

- **"Very few countries have put forward specific legislation"** for AI companions globally.
- **The EU has no specific laws for AI companions.**
- Existing frameworks that may apply:

### 5.2 Applicable EU Frameworks

| Framework | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **AI Act** | AI companions not a distinct category; may fall under Article 5 (prohibition of certain AI practices) or Article 6(2) (high-risk systems). Stakeholders call for clarification on high-risk classification. |
| **GDPR** | Applies to personal data processed by AI companions. In April 2025, Italian Supervisory Authority **fined Luka Inc. EUR 5 million** for GDPR infringements. |
| **Digital Services Act (DSA)** | Introduces obligations for VLOPs/VLOSEs. Snapchat's My AI and X's Grok designated as VLOPs. Debate over whether ChatGPT should be classified as VLOP, VLOSE, or hybrid. OpenAI reported 159.1M active monthly users (March 2026). |
| **Revised Product Liability Directive** | Extends liability to software defects and AI-caused harm. |

### 5.3 EU Parliament Actions

- **26 November 2025**: EU Parliament adopted a non-legislative report on protecting minors online:
  - Calls for **harmonised European digital age limit of 16** (default threshold)
  - Recommends an **age-verification app** and EU-harmonised approach to age verification
  - Stresses **safeguards, transparency standards**
  - Calls for **prohibition of human-like features in AI chatbots** to avoid nudging children into purchases or disengagement
  - Emphasizes importance of **AI Act enforcement**

### 5.4 International Regulatory Responses

| Jurisdiction | Action |
|---|---|
| **US** | GUARD Act and CHAT Act proposed; FTC investigation into 7 companies including OpenAI and Character Technologies Inc. |
| **China** | July 2026 regulation banning AI chatbots that encourage suicide/self-harm or manipulate mental health; requires automatic human handoff for suicide-related queries |
| **UK** | Ofcom investigation into AI companion services under Online Safety Act; age-verification systems; Teen Safety Blueprint; age-prediction model |
| **Australia** | Bans on social media for children under 16 (since March 2026); expanded age verification rules for 18-year-olds; 6 new industry codes under Online Safety Act; eSafety framework |

### 5.5 Proposed Next Steps and Recommendations

1. **UNICEF Guidance**: AI companions "should never be intentionally designed to create emotional dependency"; systems manipulating or persuading children must be prohibited.
2. **Digital Fairness Act**: EU experts propose this as a new framework to address enforcement gaps.
3. **Stronger safeguards and careful oversight specifically for children** are urgently needed.
4. Address **social, psychological, commercial, and environmental impacts** holistically.
5. Clarify whether AI companions fall under **high-risk classification** in the AI Act.
6. Address the **enforcement difficulties** with current frameworks, especially regarding private conversations and monitoring addictive design features.

## 7. Key Takeaways for Industry Analysis

- The AI companion market is experiencing **explosive growth** (88% YoY download increase, 8x increase in available apps from 2022-2025).
- **Emotional dependency** and **child safety** are the two dominant policy concerns.
- **Regulatory fragmentation** is the current reality -- no unified global or EU-specific framework exists.
- The **liability gap** is a critical legal uncertainty for providers.
- **Dark patterns** in monetization models face increasing scrutiny.
- **Energy consumption** of AI companions is an emerging environmental concern.
- Multiple jurisdictions are moving toward **age-based restrictions** (16 in EU proposal, under-16 ban in Australia, under-18 arguments from advocacy groups).
- The tension between **innovation and protection** is the central policy dilemma.
