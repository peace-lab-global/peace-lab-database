## Advancing Responsible Mental Health AI: Experts Outline Future Directions

### Workshop Overview

In late January 2026, the Delft Digital Ethics Centre (DDEC) at the Delft University of Technology (TU Delft) hosted a virtual gathering. This event served as a precursor to the 2026 India AI Impact Summit. Over thirty global specialists in ethics, public policy, mental health, and artificial intelligence attended. The World Health Organization (WHO) backed the initiative, bringing together advocates, clinicians, policymakers, and researchers.

Dr. Alain Labrique, heading the WHO's data and digital health department, emphasized that developers and stakeholders must prioritize safety when creating systems for vulnerable individuals. He stated, "As AI interacts with people in emotional vulnerability, we must ensure these systems are governed with safety and well-being."

Sameer Pujari, the WHO's lead for artificial intelligence, highlighted the rapid integration of these technologies into daily routines, which has outpaced research into psychological consequences. He noted, "We are at a critical juncture. AI adoption has far outstripped investment in understanding its mental health impact." He added that bridging this divide demands unified efforts and funding from both private and public entities.

Dr. Kenneth Carswell, representing the WHO's noncommunicable diseases and mental health division, stressed the necessity of multidisciplinary teamwork. He explained, "Minimizing risks from generative AI while maximizing benefits requires bringing together the voices of those most affected." He further noted that combining clinical knowledge, regulatory structures, and user data is essential, with the WHO dedicated to keeping user welfare central.

### Three Key Recommendations

1. **Authorities and industries must treat the utilization of generative models as a significant psychological health issue**, addressing all such tools rather than just those explicitly built for therapy.

2. **Evaluations and oversight of technological solutions must incorporate psychological health metrics.** This helps track influences on health determinants, immediate clinical results, and prolonged issues like emotional reliance. An attendee emphasized, "We need independent investments to test these effects."

3. **Developers must collaborate with psychological professionals and individuals possessing personal experience, such as adolescents, when creating support applications.** These applications must rely on solid evidence and adapt to specific cultural and linguistic contexts. Dr. Caroline Figueroa from TU Delft pointed out the critical necessity for agreed-upon crisis referral protocols and responsibility structures.

### Partner Networks as a Strategic Foundation

The event highlighted the importance of the WHO's partnership network in executing its vision for ethical technology in medicine. This framework allows the organization to leverage top-tier academic knowledge and unite global stakeholders to produce research-backed guidelines. Dr. Stefan Buijsman, the DDEC's managing director, remarked, "As a WHO Collaborating Centre, we can increase impact by collaborating with experts around the world and governments."

### Future Initiatives

The WHO is forming a global network of partner institutions across all its regions to assist member nations in adopting ethical technology. In mid-March 2026, prospective members met at TU Delft to establish common goals and initial cooperative strategies. This infrastructure aims to guarantee that medical technology governance relies on proof, moral principles, and the requirements of varied global communities.
